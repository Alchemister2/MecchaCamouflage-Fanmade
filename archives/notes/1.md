## Conclusion

For a shared, multiplayer-compatible paint state, the strongest strategy is:

**A pose-aware, multi-view, inverse-rendered dielectric camouflage texture, optimized in screen space with extra weight on the silhouette, then converted into deterministic overlapping paint strokes.**

Do not use full-body `Metallic=1, Roughness=0` as the default. Even a mathematically perfect mirror reflects a different ray than the ray that would have passed through the absent character. Curvature, grazing angles and changing viewpoints make this mismatch conspicuous.

For one known observer camera, view-dependent active camouflage is fundamentally better. It can reproduce the background along that camera’s rays. It is not, however, a globally correct solution for multiple observers sharing one replicated texture.

## Ranked strategies

For your stated multiplayer constraints:

1. **Multi-view inverse-rendered matte/dielectric atlas**
   Optimize one shared paint state over a distribution of cameras, poses, lighting conditions and distances. Use actual skinned triangles and UVs, prioritize silhouette error, and compile the result into replicated strokes.

2. **Observer-weighted dynamic atlas**
   Periodically optimize for a designated or dominant observer direction while retaining a multi-view regularizer. This can outperform a static atlas but introduces update latency, temporal flicker and conflicts between observers.

3. **Environment-class adaptive atlas**
   Select or generate a multiscale texture from the current environment class—forest, concrete, sand, snow—using matched luminance, chroma, local contrast and spatial-frequency statistics. Less exact, but stable and cheap.

4. **High-resolution imported texture used as an oracle, then compiled to strokes**
   The imported texture is not the shipping material path. It is the target image against which the stroke compiler is optimized.

5. **Selective low-gloss or metallic patches**
   Use only where rendered tests show lower error than the dielectric solution across the relevant views. Keep these patches away from silhouette regions.

6. **Full white mirror: `Metallic=1, Roughness=0`**
   Useful only in tightly controlled, low-contrast environments and nearly planar regions. It is usually the least robust option.

For a single fixed camera, strategy 2 becomes number 1.

## 1. Albedo camouflage versus mirror camouflage

The outgoing light from a surface is

[
L_o(x,v)=\int_{\Omega} f_r(x,l,v;\theta),L_i(x,l),(n\cdot l)^+,dl,
]

where the editable material parameters are

[
\theta(u)=\bigl(a_R(u),a_G(u),a_B(u),m(u),r(u)\bigr).
]

In the usual UE metallic workflow, a useful approximation is

[
F_0=(1-m),0.04+m,a,
\qquad
c_\text{diffuse}=(1-m),a.
]

Thus:

* With `Metallic=0`, Base Color primarily controls diffuse reflectance; the surface retains a roughly 4% normal-incidence dielectric specular response.
* With `Metallic=1`, the diffuse component disappears and Base Color becomes the tinted specular reflectance.
* `Roughness=0` produces a sharp mirror-like response; increasing roughness spreads the reflected energy over more directions.

UE documentation treats metallic as normally binary, and its underlying model gives metals no diffuse component while using Base Color as the incident specular color. UE also defines roughness 0 as mirror-like and roughness 1 as matte. ([Epic Games Developers][1])

Albedo camouflage attempts to choose surface reflectance so that, **after the engine lights and shades it**, the final surface resembles its background.

Mirror camouflage instead produces approximately

[
L_o(x,v)\approx F(v),
L_i!\left(x,;2(n\cdot v)n-v\right).
]

The reflected direction (2(n\cdot v)n-v) is generally not the continuation of the camera ray behind the object. Therefore a mirror is an environment reflector, not a background transmitter. A curved character acts as a collection of convex and concave mirrors, producing a distorted environment image rather than a continuation of the hidden scene.

---

## 2. When `Metallic=1, Roughness=0` helps

A mirror-like region can help when all of the following are approximately true:

* The surface is planar or has slowly varying normals.
* The reflected environment is close in color and brightness to the occluded background.
* The observer viewpoint is tightly constrained.
* The surrounding environment has low spatial complexity—for example fog, uniform sky or a large flat wall.
* Reflection data are stable and complete for the relevant direction.
* The region is not near the projected silhouette.

It makes the object more visible when:

* Surface normals vary strongly, causing reflection distortion.
* Bright sky, lights or emissive objects are reflected into a darker background.
* The camera or character moves, creating moving highlights.
* Screen-space reflection coverage changes and the engine switches to a capture or other fallback.
* The region reaches a silhouette or grazing angle.
* Different observers see different reflected directions.
* Reflected features move in the wrong direction relative to background parallax.

Schlick Fresnel is approximately

[
F=F_0+(1-F_0)(1-v\cdot h)^5.
]

At grazing angles, the response approaches an achromatic value near 1. Even a dark metal can therefore acquire a pale or white-looking rim. Disney’s principled model explicitly notes that grazing specular remains achromatic. ([Disney Animation][2])

A selective mirror algorithm is reasonable: solve both the best dielectric and best metallic result for each coarse region, then use metallic only where its multi-view tail error is lower by a significant margin. Do not use continuous metallic as a brightness control; use a binary mask except where it represents unresolved mixed coverage.

## 3. Why dark colors become washed out or white

There are several independent causes.

### sRGB values are not linear reflectance values

For an sRGB code value (c_s), the linear value is

[
c_\ell=
\begin{cases}
c_s/12.92,&c_s\leq 0.04045,\
\left(\frac{c_s+0.055}{1.055}\right)^{2.4},&c_s>0.04045.
\end{cases}
]

For example:

[
0.2_\text{sRGB}\rightarrow 0.0331_\text{linear}.
]

If `0.2` is accidentally interpreted as a linear Base Color, it corresponds to approximately `0.485` after sRGB encoding before lighting. That is a large brightness error.

The correct requirement is **exactly one sRGB decode** between the sampled display color and the linear Base Color seen by the shader. Some paint APIs expect encoded byte values because the texture is later sampled as sRGB; others expect linear float values. Measure the contract rather than assuming it. Metallic and roughness must remain linear mask data, not sRGB-decoded color data. UE’s texture settings explicitly distinguish sRGB color data from mask channels. ([Epic Games Developers][3])

### Final scene color is not Base Color

A sampled background pixel already contains:

* illumination,
* shadowing,
* diffuse response,
* specular response,
* atmospheric effects,
* exposure,
* tone mapping,
* bloom,
* color grading.

Copying this final sRGB value into Base Color causes the engine to illuminate and process it again. It is not an inverse of the rendering process.

### White metallic Base Color is white specular reflectance

With `Metallic=1`, white Base Color does not mean “white diffuse paint.” It means a highly reflective white conductor with essentially no diffuse component. Bright environment reflections dominate the result.

### Tone mapping pushes high-energy values toward white

UE tone mapping converts HDR scene color to display output. Bright, saturated values become progressively lighter and can approach white as the sensor/film response saturates. Exposure changes further alter this mapping. ([Epic Games Developers][4])

### Pre-exposure can invalidate comparisons between frames

UE can apply the previous frame’s exposure inside shaders before writing scene color. If visible-character and hidden-character captures use different exposure histories, direct HDR subtraction is invalid unless pre-exposure is normalized or exposure is locked. ([Epic Games Developers][5])

### Roughness may be decoded incorrectly

A roughness value of `0.5` incorrectly treated as sRGB becomes approximately `0.214` linear, making the surface much glossier than intended. The resulting sharp highlights frequently appear as white dots or broad washed-out regions.

## 4. Preserve or override material properties?

Preserve the original **shading model**, normal maps and geometry-dependent behavior. Do not necessarily preserve the original metallic and roughness values.

A physical layer of camouflage paint over metal is normally a dielectric coating. The outermost visible layer should therefore be:

[
m=0.
]

Use `Metallic=1` only for genuinely exposed metal. If the paint represents pigment, cloth, skin, polymer or mud, metallic should be zero regardless of the substrate beneath it.

Roughness should be overridden toward the coating’s desired response. For robust camouflage, moderately high roughness usually reduces sharp, moving highlights. It does not remove specular energy; it spreads it, so the exact value should still be selected by rendered error rather than set blindly to 1.

If the existing material has clear coat, cloth sheen, subsurface scattering or a custom specular term that the paint API cannot control, those components become residual error. Capture the shading model and relevant buffers so that this is diagnosed rather than misattributed to Base Color.

## 5. The mathematically correct objective

For view/pose/lighting sample (i), let

[
B_i(p)=R(\text{scene without character};i,p)
]

be the unobstructed background at screen pixel (p), and let

[
C_i(p;\theta)=R(\text{scene with painted character};i,p)
]

be the rendered character result.

The target background is the value along the **same observer-camera ray**, not a world-space sample taken along the surface normal.

A suitable optimization is

[
\begin{aligned}
\theta^*=\arg\min_{\theta\in\mathcal C}
\sum_i \pi_i\sum_{p\in\Omega_i}w_i(p)
\bigg[
&\lambda_Y
\rho!\left(
\log(Y(C_i)+\epsilon)-\log(Y(B_i)+\epsilon)
\right)\
+&\lambda_C D_\text{perc}!\left(T(C_i),T(B_i)\right)\
+&\lambda_G
\left|\nabla T(C_i)-\nabla T(B_i)\right|*1
\bigg]\
+&\lambda_E L*\text{silhouette}
+\lambda_T L_\text{temporal}
+\lambda_S L_\text{stroke}.
\end{aligned}
]

Here:

* (T) is the exact display transform used by the game.
* (Y) is luminance.
* (D_\text{perc}) is a perceptual image error.
* (L_\text{silhouette}) emphasizes boundary contrast and coherent outline edges.
* (L_\text{temporal}) penalizes flicker and large paint changes.
* (L_\text{stroke}) enforces the paint API’s resolution and stroke budget.
* (\mathcal C) imposes physically reasonable metallic and roughness constraints.

Use scene-linear pre-tonemap data for the inverse-lighting part and final post-tonemap data for the perceptual part. Final color alone is nonlinear and ambiguous; GBuffer data alone do not describe the outgoing appearance.

For multiplayer, optimize the expected error plus a tail term such as a high percentile or conditional value-at-risk. A pure average can hide the character well from most views while producing one extremely conspicuous side or rear view.

### Efficient Base Color inversion

For fixed pose, view, lighting, `Metallic=0` and candidate roughness (r), the pre-tonemap response is approximately affine in Base Color:

[
C(a;r)\approx S(r)+A(r)a.
]

Capture four calibration renders using the same material and paint path:

[
S=C((0,0,0);r),
]

[
A=
\begin{bmatrix}
C((1,0,0);r)-S &
C((0,1,0);r)-S &
C((0,0,1);r)-S
\end{bmatrix}.
]

Then solve

[
a^*=
\arg\min_{0\leq a\leq1}
\left|W\left(Aa+S-B\right)\right|_2^2
+\lambda|a-a_0|_2^2.
]

The simpler channel-independent approximation is

[
a_k=
\operatorname{clamp}
\left(
\frac{B_k-S_k}{C_{\text{white},k}-S_k},
0,1
\right).
]

The black probe estimates the residual dielectric specular and non-Base-Color contribution. White minus black estimates the diffuse transport.

Run these probes in an offline calibration map or on an offscreen clone using the same existing material and paint API. No final material swap is required.

Because tone mapping and some GI effects are nonlinear, render the solved result and perform one or more finite-difference or coordinate-descent corrections. Search roughness over a small candidate set, solve albedo for each, and choose the candidate minimizing the final multi-view loss.

The probes also reveal feasibility. If the desired background lies outside the attainable range between the dark and bright probe responses, exact matching is impossible with passive Albedo/Metallic/Roughness. This commonly happens against bright emissive objects, sky or deep shadows.

## 6. Exact projection for a curved skeletal mesh

Do not use separate planar front, side and back projections as the final mapping. Those produce stretching, hard transitions and incorrect curves. Front, rear and side captures should be treated as observations of the same UV surface.

For bind-pose vertex (x_j^0), the skinned component-space position is

[
x_j^c(t)=
\sum_b w_{jb},
T_b(t),T_{b,\text{bind}}^{-1}
\begin{bmatrix}x_j^0\1\end{bmatrix}.
]

Transform into world space and then clip space:

[
h=P_iV_iM_\text{component}
\begin{bmatrix}x_j^c\1\end{bmatrix}.
]

Conceptually,

[
u_s=\tfrac12(h_x/h_w+1),
\qquad
v_s=\tfrac12(1-h_y/h_w).
]

In production, use the engine’s view/projection helpers rather than manually reproducing UE’s coordinate conventions, reversed depth, viewport subrects and temporal jitter.

Two accurate implementations are possible.

### Atlas-texel pull method

Offline, rasterize every UV triangle into an atlas lookup containing:

* triangle index,
* barycentric coordinates,
* material section,
* geometric area,
* seam identifier,
* canonical normal.

At runtime:

1. Skin the three vertices of the stored triangle.
2. Reconstruct the texel’s world position and normal using barycentric interpolation.
3. Project into each observer capture.
4. Reject back-facing, occluded and self-occluded samples.
5. Sample the hidden-character background at that screen coordinate.
6. Accumulate the observation into the atlas optimization.

This guarantees atlas coverage and is usually the more efficient method.

### Screen-pixel push method

For every pixel in the projected character bounds:

1. Construct the camera ray.
2. Intersect it with the current skinned-triangle BVH.
3. Select the nearest triangle.
4. Recover barycentric coordinates.
5. Interpolate its UV.
6. Sample the hidden-character background at the same pixel.
7. Splat the result into the atlas with an elliptical footprint.

If screen-space triangle rasterization is used instead of ray intersection, UV interpolation must be perspective-correct:

[
\lambda_i'=
\frac{\lambda_i/w_i}
{\sum_j\lambda_j/w_j},
\qquad
uv=\sum_i\lambda_i'uv_i.
]

Visibility must account for both:

* nearest character surface, to reject self-occluded back surfaces;
* scene depth, to reject character points hidden behind scene geometry.

For multiple views, use continuous weights rather than hard region boundaries:

[
w_i(u)=
V_i(u),
P_i(u),
q_i,
\bigl[\max(n(u)\cdot v_i,\tau)\bigr]^\gamma,
]

where (V_i) is visibility, (P_i) is projected screen footprint and (q_i) is the prior probability of that observer/view.

Verify that the paint UV channel is non-overlapping. Mirrored or stacked UV islands force separate body surfaces to share the same painted value, making independent front/rear matching mathematically impossible. Also verify UV correspondence across LODs.

For the two body variants, build separate lookup data and usually separate optimized atlases. Share an atlas only when topology, section layout and paint UVs are actually equivalent.

UE exposes CPU evaluation of skinned vertex positions in component space, though compressed access can be slow. Enabling general CPU skinning is documented as expensive, so cache reference-to-local matrices and batch only the needed vertices instead of enabling it globally. UVs and tangents can be obtained from the static vertex buffers when CPU access is retained. ([Epic Games Developers][6])

## 7. Is view-dependent camouflage fundamentally better?

For one observer, yes.

The desired appearance is actually a function

[
C^*(u,v,\text{pose},t,\text{lighting}),
]

while a static paint texture stores only

[
\theta(u).
]

If the same surface texel needs two different colors for two observers, no single static value can satisfy both. Metallic and roughness add constrained BRDF view dependence, but they cannot encode an arbitrary outgoing image for every direction.

A camera-targeted active texture can therefore approach exact screen matching for one observer. It remains limited by:

* silhouette and depth,
* cast shadows,
* opacity,
* background parallax,
* paint update bandwidth,
* animation deformation,
* exposure latency,
* conflicting observers.

In multiplayer, one replicated paint state must use an agreed observer distribution. A useful compromise is an observer-weighted robust atlas updated only when the dominant view changes materially, with temporal regularization and hysteresis. Updating every frame can itself create visible flicker and motion.

## 8. Engine data to capture

| Priority | Data                                            | Purpose                                                      |
| -------- | ----------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1        | Background-only pre-tonemap HDR scene color     | Scene-linear target for inverse shading                      |
| 2        | Background-only final post-tonemap color        | Actual player-visible perceptual target                      |
| 3        | Background scene depth                          | Scene occlusion and parallax checks                          |
| 4        | Character depth and mask/custom stencil         | Exact projected silhouette and self-visibility               |
| 5        | Character world normal                          | Reflection, grazing-angle and projection diagnostics         |
| 6        | Character Base Color, Metallic and Roughness    | Verify that paint data reached the GBuffer correctly         |
| 7        | Shading-model ID and relevant material buffers  | Detect uncontrolled sheen, clear coat or subsurface response |
| 8        | Velocity                                        | Temporal warping and motion-error metrics                    |
| 9        | View/projection matrices, viewport, FOV, jitter | Exact pixel correspondence                                   |
| 10       | Exposure and pre-exposure                       | Normalize scene color between captures                       |
| 11       | Optional black/R/G/B probe captures             | Estimate local material-to-image transport                   |
| 12       | Reflection/specular diagnostics                 | Explain metallic failures, not define the primary target     |

UE scene captures expose HDR scene color, final color, scene depth, normals and Base Color. Deferred scene textures also expose Metallic, Roughness, World Normal, Velocity and shading-related buffers. High-resolution diagnostics can export pre-tonemap and post-tonemap HDR color, depth, Base Color, Metallic, Roughness and World Normal. GBuffer sampling is not available in the forward renderer. ([Epic Games Developers][7])

Use the same camera, exposure and post-processing settings for visible and hidden captures. TAA moves the scene by subpixel offsets and combines history, while viewport UV and scene-texture UV may address different extents. Either fit with jitter disabled and evaluate with TAA enabled, or record and reproduce the exact jitter/history conditions. ([Epic Games Developers][8])

Reflection captures, SSR or Lumen data are useful for explaining a bad mirror result. They are not a substitute for capturing the actual rendered output.

## 9. Algorithm when only paint channels are available

Assume runtime CPU triangle queries are unavailable but offline mesh data can be prepared.

1. **Calibrate the paint path.**
   Paint known ramps into a hidden test mesh. Inspect Base Color, Metallic and Roughness buffers. Determine whether Albedo commands are encoded sRGB or linear and verify that masks remain linear.

2. **Create variant-specific offline lookup assets.**
   Store UV-to-triangle/barycentric maps, front/rear/side masks, seam pairs and canonical normals for each body variant.

3. **Capture the unobstructed environment.**
   Use a small set of cameras representing probable observers. Capture both pre-tonemap HDR and final color.

4. **Fix metallic to zero.**
   Unless a region is intentionally exposed metal, use `Metallic=0`.

5. **Search roughness coarsely.**
   Start with candidates such as `0.65, 0.75, 0.85, 0.95`.

6. **Solve Base Color using calibrated inverse transport.**
   Use black/R/G/B probe response data where available. Otherwise use a region-specific transfer LUT learned in representative lighting conditions.

7. **Use robust regional aggregation.**
   When exact pose projection is unavailable, use background medians or Huber means from corresponding front/rear/side camera regions, not single pixels. Preserve large-scale variation and discard unstable high-frequency detail.

8. **Apply a separate silhouette objective.**
   Low-pass the target near edges, raise roughness and prioritize local boundary luminance over exact interior texture.

9. **Render and correct.**
   Compare final character color with hidden-character background and perform tile-level finite-difference corrections.

10. **Compile to deterministic strokes.**

If neither runtime geometry nor an offline UV-to-surface map exists, accurate projection onto a curved animated surface is unavailable. The remaining solution is coarse region-based camouflage, not exact background reprojection.

## 10. Algorithm with CPU mesh raycast and skinning

A practical pipeline is:

```text
for each sampled camera, pose and lighting state:
    capture background-only HDR, final color and depth
    skin the vertices used by the current render LOD
    refit the skinned-triangle BVH

    for each pixel in the character's screen bounds:
        ray = camera_ray(pixel)
        hit = nearest_skinned_triangle(ray)
        if no hit:
            continue

        uv = barycentric_interpolate(hit.triangle_uvs)
        target = background_HDR[pixel]
        accumulate target into UV atlas using screen/UV footprint
```

Then:

1. Solve Base Color for each roughness candidate.
2. Render the candidate through the real material.
3. Evaluate final color, boundary and temporal losses.
4. Choose the lowest robust multi-view result.
5. Generate stroke parameters from the atlas.
6. Replay the strokes through the real paint API and measure the round-trip result.

Performance measures:

* Skin only vertices referenced by visible or candidate triangles.
* Cache bone reference-to-local matrices.
* Refit a BVH rather than rebuilding its topology.
* Use a lower update frequency than rendering frequency.
* Use the exact current render LOD, or maintain validated LOD transfer maps.
* Include morph targets and cloth deformation when they materially change the visible surface.

## 11. Human visual perception and the objective

### Silhouette and edges

The silhouette is often more important than interior RGB accuracy. A coherent, continuous boundary identifies the character even when its average color matches. Stationary-target camouflage experiments found an orientation-sensitive edge-disruption measure to be the strongest predictor in that dataset; local luminance and feature/pattern similarity also contributed. High-contrast markings that intersect an outline can impede shape recognition, but arbitrary contrast added near an edge can instead increase visibility. ([Springer][9])

Use an explicit boundary band in screen space. Penalize:

* luminance discontinuity across the silhouette,
* chromatic discontinuity,
* edge energy parallel to the character outline,
* long coherent contour segments.

Reward background edges that appear to continue across the object or false edges that cross the outline, but only when they remain consistent with the local background.

### Brightness and color

Match both luminance and chromatic contrast. Human detection data support contributions from both luminance and color contrast rather than treating hue matching as sufficient. ([Royal Society Publishing][10])

Use log-luminance or Weber-like contrast rather than raw RGB error:

[
E_Y=
\left|
\log(Y_C+\epsilon)-\log(Y_B+\epsilon)
\right|.
]

This better reflects the significance of relative brightness differences in dark and bright regions.

### Texture frequency

Match texture at the spatial scales that remain visible at the expected observer distance. High-frequency detail that projects below pixel Nyquist becomes aliasing, shimmer or an averaged color. Use a Laplacian or Gabor pyramid and match the local energy in each useful band.

A good atlas normally contains:

* low-frequency regional luminance matching,
* medium-scale disruptive structure,
* limited high-frequency detail for close views.

### Motion

Motion creates a strong segmentation cue that material color cannot eliminate. Research on moving camouflage targets finds that movement rate, local contrast and spatial frequency interact in determining detectability; camouflage may still impede identification even when movement reveals the target’s location. ([PubMed][11])

Therefore:

* minimize temporal paint flicker,
* preserve low local contrast while moving,
* avoid sharp specular points that move over the body,
* evaluate animation and camera movement, not only still frames,
* treat cast shadows and disocclusion as separate residual cues.

## 12. Avoiding edge, curve and dotted-paint artifacts

For geometry and projection:

* Use skinned-triangle projection, not box or triplanar projection.
* Blend views continuously; never create hard front/side/back material borders.
* Reject samples across depth discontinuities.
* Use a character depth pass or nearest ray hit for self-occlusion.
* Give grazing samples lower projection confidence but retain a separate, heavily weighted silhouette loss.
* Constrain corresponding UV-seam vertices to compatible colors.
* Dilate every UV island sufficiently for the lowest mip that may be sampled.
* Match the render LOD and paint lookup LOD.
* Detect stacked or mirrored paint UVs before optimization.

For paint resolution:

* Measure the brush’s point-spread function.
* Low-pass the target to the paint system’s achievable bandwidth.
* Keep stroke-center spacing below approximately half the brush’s effective full-width at half-maximum for smooth coverage.
* Use 50–75% footprint overlap for soft brushes.
* Place fine strokes using blue-noise rather than a regular grid.
* Paint large-scale structure first and fit residual detail with progressively smaller strokes.
* Generate or simulate mip levels before evaluating.
* Avoid roughness dithering; it creates sparkling specular variation.
* Use color dithering only when the spatial frequency will integrate at the intended distance.

The target texture should be prefiltered rather than attempting to retain detail that the stroke system cannot reproduce.

## 13. Converting a high-resolution atlas to paint strokes

Model the paint API as an ordered raster operator:

[
P(S)=P\left({q_k,r_k,c_k,\alpha_k,\text{channel}*k}*{k=1}^N\right).
]

For conventional alpha-over painting, one stroke approximately performs

[
T_{k+1}(u)=
\bigl(1-\alpha_kK_k(u)\bigr)T_k(u)
+\alpha_kK_k(u)c_k.
]

Solve

[
S^*=
\arg\min_S
|W(P(S)-T_\text{atlas})|*2^2
+\lambda_N|S|
+\lambda_E L*\text{edge}
+\lambda_\text{net}C_\text{replication}.
]

A practical compiler:

1. Record the exact brush falloff, blend operator and quantization.
2. Prefilter the target according to the measured brush bandwidth.
3. Initialize with large strokes fitted to quadtree or superpixel regions.
4. Render the strokes through a software simulation of the real paint API.
5. Compute the residual.
6. Greedily add the stroke giving the largest weighted residual reduction.
7. Refine nearby stroke color, radius and opacity.
8. Continue coarse-to-fine until the stroke budget or error threshold is reached.
9. Replay the final sequence through the actual game API.
10. Optimize against that round-trip image, not against the ideal atlas alone.

For replication:

* use deterministic stroke ordering,
* quantize positions, radii and channel values,
* replicate a fixed seed when using blue-noise placement,
* checksum the reconstructed channel textures,
* store triangle ID and barycentric location when possible rather than an unstable world-space point.

## Recommended channel values

There are no scene-independent exact Albedo RGB values. The mathematically correct Albedo is the constrained inverse solution. Fixed RGB recommendations would ignore illumination, exposure and background radiance.

The following are robust starting constraints for a nonmetal camouflage coating:

| Region relative to an observer                        | Albedo                                            | Metallic | Roughness default | Search interval |
| ----------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | -------: | ----------------: | --------------: |
| Front-facing, (n\cdot v\geq0.65)                      | Inverse-rendered front-view target                |   `0.00` |            `0.75` |     `0.65–0.85` |
| Side/oblique, (0.20\leq n\cdot v<0.65)                | Robust side-view target, slightly lower-frequency |   `0.00` |            `0.85` |     `0.75–0.95` |
| Rear body region                                      | Inverse-rendered rear-view target                 |   `0.00` |            `0.78` |     `0.68–0.88` |
| Silhouette/grazing, (n\cdot v<0.20), or boundary band | Low-pass local background plus edge objective     |   `0.00` |            `0.95` |     `0.88–1.00` |

Additional constraints:

* Start with Base Color luminance in approximately the physically plausible dielectric range `0.02–0.80` linear; expand only when calibration shows that it improves final error.
* Avoid exact Base Color `1.0` unless matching very high-reflectance material such as snow under appropriate illumination. UE’s measured examples range from about `0.02` for charcoal/asphalt to `0.81` for fresh snow. ([Epic Games Developers][1])
* For exposed metal: `Metallic=1`, start at `Roughness=0.65`, search `0.55–0.85`.
* For an experimental mirror patch: `Metallic=1`, `Roughness=0.02–0.08`, only on near-planar interior regions whose predicted multi-view reflection error is lower than the dielectric result.
* Never place full mirror behavior in the silhouette band.

“Front,” “side” and “back” should define capture/view sets, not abrupt texture partitions. A rear surface seen by a rear observer is front-facing with respect to that observer.

| Base Color looks correct in Unlit but wrong in Lit     | Final background color copied without inverse lighting     | Compare Unlit, Lighting Only and pre-tonemap captures | Use black/R/G/B transport probes               |
| Dark paint has white rims                              | Fresnel plus low roughness or metallic                     | Inspect World Normal, Roughness and grazing angle     | `Metallic=0`, raise edge roughness             |
| Moving white points                                    | Glossy roughness, bad mask encoding or roughness dithering | View Roughness buffer and temporal p99 luminance      | Keep roughness linear and spatially smooth     |
| Front view works, sides stretch                        | Planar projection or hard directional masks                | Visualize triangle/UV correspondence                  | Use skinned barycentric projection             |
| Rear region shows front texture                        | Stacked/mirrored UVs                                       | UV overlap visualization                              | Unique paint UVs or accept coupled surfaces    |
| Curves have discontinuous bands                        | Hard front/side/back classification                        | Plot blending weights                                 | Continuous normal/view weighting               |
| Texture has dots or holes                              | Brush spacing exceeds footprint bandwidth                  | Render accumulated coverage only                      | More overlap, larger brushes, prefilter target |
| Seams appear at distance                               | Insufficient gutters or mip bleed                          | Force successive mip levels                           | Seam constraints and base-level dilation       |
| Correct when static, unstable when moving              | TAA jitter, LOD switching or update flicker                | Lock LOD/TAA and compare                              | Stable lookup, temporal regularization         |
| Hidden and visible captures have different brightness  | Auto-exposure or pre-exposure mismatch                     | Log exposure values per capture                       | Lock or normalize exposure                     |
| One body variant works and the other does not          | Reused topology/UV correspondence                          | Display triangle and UV IDs                           | Separate variant lookup/atlas                  |
| Works in editor but not shipping                       | CPU render data unavailable or different LOD               | Validate cooked build buffers                         | Bake compact lookup data into a runtime asset  |
| Camouflage surface matches but target is still obvious | Shadow, silhouette motion or depth cue                     | Compare body-mask and full-frame metrics              | Treat as a geometry/shadow limitation          |

UE’s Unlit mode displays Base Color without lighting, while Lighting Only and Detail Lighting help separate material coloration from lighting and normal effects. ([Epic Games Developers][12])

## Success metrics

Use several complementary metrics.

1. **Human visual-search performance**
   Randomized target-present/absent trials; record detection time, hit rate, false alarms and

   [
   d'=Z(\text{hit rate})-Z(\text{false-alarm rate}).
   ]

   This is the most directly relevant endpoint.

2. **Boundary-weighted perceptual image error**
   Run FLIP on the final rendered image versus the hidden-character reference, with elevated weights near the silhouette. FLIP is specifically intended to compare rendered images and generate perceptually oriented difference maps with viewing-condition dependence. ([NVIDIA Developer][13])

3. **Log-luminance error**
   Report mean, 95th percentile and 99th percentile within the character mask and silhouette band.

4. **Local boundary contrast**
   Sample inward and outward along the silhouette normal and measure luminance/chroma discontinuity.

5. **Edge-orientation metric**
   Measure coherent edge energy parallel to the silhouette and false/background-continuing edge energy across it.

6. **Multiscale texture error**
   Compare local bandpass or Gabor energy across several spatial-frequency bands.

7. **Specular-outlier rate**
   Fraction of character pixels exceeding the local background by a chosen relative luminance threshold; also report temporal p99.

8. **Temporal error**
   Warp consecutive frames using character velocity and measure residual flicker, highlight motion and update discontinuities.

9. **Robustness statistics**
   Report median, p90, p99 and worst case over views, poses, environments, exposure states and graphics settings.

10. **Operational cost**
    Stroke count, replicated bytes, paint-update CPU/GPU cost, reconstruction determinism and update latency.

CIEDE2000 can supplement final SDR color comparisons in diffuse regions, but it should not be the only measure: the CIE standard notes limitations for stimuli perceived as primary emitters or specular reflections. ([CIE][14])

The primary benchmark should compare at least three baselines: original material, full mirror, and the multi-view dielectric optimizer. The expected result is that the mirror occasionally wins in a narrow view while the dielectric inverse-rendered solution has substantially lower tail error across varied views.
